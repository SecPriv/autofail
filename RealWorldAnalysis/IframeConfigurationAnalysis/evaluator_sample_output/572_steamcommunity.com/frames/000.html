TL;DR: A flawed but enjoyable open-world action RPG carried mostly by Team Ninja’s excellent combat system. The story, visuals, open-world structure, and PC optimization could be better, but the weapon variety and fighting mechanics make it worth playing if you enjoy samurai action games.

Rise of the Ronin is one of those games I can recommend while fully understanding why many people have mixed feelings about it. It is not Team Ninja at their absolute best in every area, but when the combat clicks, the game becomes very easy to enjoy.

The strongest part of the game is definitely the combat. Team Ninja knows how to make fighting feel satisfying, and that is still true here. There are many weapon types, multiple combat styles, counters, stance-based decisions, and enough variety to keep encounters interesting for a long time. If you enjoyed the combat depth and weapon variety in games like Nioh, you will probably find a lot to like here as well. It is not exactly the same kind of game, but the Team Ninja DNA is obvious in the best way.

The setting is also a big part of the appeal. The game takes place in a fascinating period of Japanese history, and even if the execution is not always perfect, I liked exploring this version of Japan. Visually, the game is inconsistent, but there are still many beautiful locations, landscapes, temples, streets, forests, and quiet natural moments that capture the beauty of Japan surprisingly well. It is not a graphical showcase, but it can still look very pleasant in the right scenes.

That said, the game definitely has issues.

The story could have been stronger. The historical backdrop is interesting, and some major characters are well-designed and memorable, but the writing does not always give the plot enough emotional weight. There are choices, factions, and important historical figures, but the narrative often feels less powerful than it should. It has good moments, but it rarely reaches the level of drama or character depth that the setting deserves.

The presentation is also uneven. Important story characters usually look good, and some of them are genuinely well-made, but facial animation and general character expression can feel stiff or outdated. This hurts some dramatic scenes, because the game clearly wants you to care about its characters, but the animation does not always support that emotion.

The open world is another mixed point. There is a lot to do, but not all of it feels equally meaningful. Some activities and missions can become repetitive, and the structure sometimes feels more like a checklist than a truly organic adventure. The traversal is enjoyable and the world has charm, but the overall design is not as strong as the combat.

Performance is also worth mentioning. My experience was stable, but the PC version clearly has a mixed reputation for a reason. Many players have reported optimization problems, stuttering, and inconsistent performance, so I would not blindly recommend it to everyone without checking how it runs on your hardware first.

Overall, I still enjoyed Rise of the Ronin. It has flaws, and some of them are hard to ignore, but the combat carries the experience. The weapon variety, fighting styles, historical setting, and occasional beautiful views of Japan make it worth playing, especially if you are already a fan of Team Ninja’s action games.

Recommended, but mainly for players who value combat systems more than story, graphics, or polished open-world design.

TL;DR: A strange, atmospheric mix of idle/incremental progression, dungeon crawling, autobattler elements, and light puzzle mechanics. Not for everyone, but very enjoyable if you like slow-burn indie games with a dark mood.

Horripilant is a small but surprisingly engaging game with a very specific identity. It combines incremental progression, resource gathering, combat, exploration, and a mysterious underground atmosphere into something that feels unusual in a good way.

The strongest part of the game is its mood. It does not rely on cheap scares, but instead builds a quiet, unsettling feeling as you descend deeper and slowly uncover more of its systems. The gameplay loop is simple at first, but it becomes satisfying once the upgrades, battles, and progression start connecting together.

It is definitely not a game for everyone. If you dislike idle mechanics, repetition, or slower pacing, you might bounce off it. But if you enjoy niche indie games that experiment with different genres and reward patience, Horripilant is easy to recommend.

Overall, I had a good time with it. It is weird, atmospheric, and charming in its own dark way.

Recommended, especially for fans of incremental games, dungeon crawlers, and unusual indie titles.
